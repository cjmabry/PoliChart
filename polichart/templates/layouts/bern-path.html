We did the math.

The State of the Race

As of March 29, there have been 2,304 pledged delegates awarded - or 57% of those available.

1,747 pledged delegates are up for grabs in the remaining contests.

Hillary Clinton currently leads Bernie Sanders by 228 pledged delegates.

2,026 pledged delegates are required to win a majority.

The Goal

To finish with a pledged majority, Bernie needs to win 58% of the remaining pledged delegates after March 15.

Bernie's camp argues that superdelegates will switch to whichever candidate holds a pledged majority, as they did in 2008. For this reason, they are not included in these calculations.

So How's He Doing?

As of March 29, Bernie is 8% over his 58% target.

The Road Ahead

While Hillary Clinton is still the clear front-runner in the Democratic nomination, the race moves into the most favorable stretch for the Vermont senator after March 15. Clintonâ€™s lead will most likely shrink until June 14, but the real question is by how much.

Should Bernie roughly meet the targets above - by no means an easy task - he will be on track to win a majority of pledged delegates, and the Democratic contest could take until June 14 to be decided.

Some margins, like those in smaller delegate contests like Guam and Wyoming, are less important, as percentages can swing wildly and only a few delegates will change hands.

Others, like New York and California, have massive delegate hauls, and a margin change of just a few points could affect the delegate count. For this reason, these bigger states, including Washington, Pennsylvania, New Jersey, and Maryland, are of much greater importance.

It's hard to see a path forward for Sanders without New York, for example, and it's virtually impossible for him to secure the nomination if he doesn't win California.

In the end, the most important metric to watch is if Bernie can secure 58% of the pledged delegates after March 15. If he can, he'll finish with a pledged majority, regardless of how close he gets to these targets.
